Section 523(a) -- What Debts Survive Bankruptcy?

You are filing bankruptcy -- or thinking about it -- and you have one question: will my debt actually be wiped out? This is the free, plain-language guide to the federal law that decides.

The short answer: Most debts are dischargeable. Credit card balances, medical bills, personal loans, past-due utilities, and old judgments are typically wiped out. But 19 specific categories of debt survive bankruptcy under Section 523(a). This page explains every one of them.

How Section 523(a) Works

When you receive a bankruptcy discharge, it acts as a permanent court order prohibiting creditors from collecting discharged debts. But Section 523(a) of the Bankruptcy Code (11 U.S.C. Section 523(a)) carves out specific exceptions -- debts that survive the discharge and remain legally enforceable.

There are two types of exceptions:

  • Self-executing exceptions -- these apply automatically, without the creditor doing anything. Examples: tax debts, student loans, child support, DUI judgments.
  • Complaint-required exceptions -- the creditor must file an adversary proceeding (a lawsuit within the bankruptcy case) within 60 days of the first date set for the 341 meeting. If the creditor misses that deadline, the debt is discharged. Examples: fraud, embezzlement, willful injury.

The 60-day deadline matters enormously. If a creditor believes your debt was obtained through fraud but fails to file an adversary proceeding within 60 days of the 341 meeting, that debt is discharged -- even if fraud actually occurred. This deadline is strictly enforced.

Chapter 7 vs. Chapter 13: Key Differences

Both chapters share the same nondischargeable debt categories under Section 523(a). But they handle them differently:

Chapter 7

In a Chapter 7 liquidation, the discharge typically arrives in about 4 months. Nondischargeable debts simply survive -- you still owe the full balance after the case ends. There is no payment plan and no mechanism to catch up on arrears within the case.

Chapter 13

Chapter 13 provides a 3-to-5-year payment plan. While the same debts are nondischargeable, the plan lets you structure payments on those obligations. Priority debts like tax claims and domestic support arrears can be paid through the plan at 0% interest. This is often the strategic reason to choose Chapter 13 even when Chapter 7 is available.

The "super discharge" is mostly gone. Before 2005, Chapter 13 could discharge many debts that Chapter 7 could not (property settlement debts, certain fraud claims, etc.). The BAPCPA amendments in 2005 eliminated most of those differences. Today, the discharge scope is nearly identical between chapters.

What Happens If a Creditor Tries to Collect a Discharged Debt?

Once your discharge order is entered, the discharge injunction under 11 U.S.C. Section 524 permanently prohibits creditors from collecting discharged debts. If a creditor contacts you about a discharged debt, that is a violation of federal law.

However, if a debt is genuinely nondischargeable under Section 523(a), the creditor retains full collection rights. That is exactly why understanding these exceptions matters -- before you file.
